Fast Fight

"Fast Fight" is an NC State pep band tradition dating back several years. During the second-to-last media time out of each home men's and women's basketball game, the trumpet section takes the court, running to both of the sidelines (bounded by the throw-in lines) and also usually the endline opposite the band, if time permits. As the 5-minute mark of the final period approaches, the trumpets gather in the corner beneath the bleachers where the band sits. Once the timeout is called, the drum set plays a spastic melody as the trumpets run out on each side, with one or two more experienced section members (depending on size of the section that day; chosen on an ad-hoc basis) taking a knee in front of the group selected to count the group off. While on the sides, the trumpets play a short tune which usually has two short downward-modulating tunes, followed by two short upward-modulating tunes. During the downward-modulating sections, the student section claps along to the beat, and during the upward-modulating ones, the students clap and say "woo!" on the peak of the modulation. While all this is going on, the cheerleaders get on each others shoulders and run along but behind the trumpets, with the uppermost cheerleader moving their pom-poms with the students.

After that, the band goes to the corner they started in and plays slow fight the same way as before all basketball and football games: once with only the trumpets, playing slowly at first but accelerating exponentially as the song goes on, followed by a second repitition with the entire band joining in. During this part, the student section claps along in rhythm, clapping with themselves once followed by a shared clap with whoever is on their left or right side.

Legend passed down from members of Mu Beta Psi allege that this tradition started when the basketball coach Norm Sloan complained that the fight song was too quick and unremarkable, and that we needed something faster to get the students into the game; in response, the trumpet section played the first song as fast as possible. From there, it has gradually devolved into some unrecognizable short trills up and down. That version of the story places the advent of the tradition to some time between 1966 to 1980.

This may be a case were the legend is more entertaining than reality, though, if an article published in The Technician in 1988 is to be believed. That story, as recounted by pep band director Bruce "Chip" Spainhour, is that trumpet players were messing around during a football game, trying to see who could play the fight song the fastest. There's not much explanation of how or why, but eventually, the band started playing it with the cheerleaders, playing at that time to all 4 sides of the crowd. "The band got together with the cheerleaders and did it," Spainhour explained (The Technician, March 16th, 1988, p. 9).
