Carter-Finley Stadium has been used by NC State since 1966; it replaced Riddick Field.

As the 1950s turned to the 1960s, NC State's historic home for football, Riddick Stadium (opened 1906), was becoming increasingly outmoded. The stadium, whose permanent seating capacity remained below 15,000 for its entire life, was dilapidated, old, and -- worst of all -- small. The Wolfpack played just 3 games at home every season from 1957 through 1965 (except 1965 -- we'll get to that), and played only two games at home in 1963. Since 1943, the annual game against North Carolina had been held in Raleigh just twice: once in 1955, and once in 1965, with the second game largely being scheduled under the expectation of the Wolfpack having a new stadium. Concurrently, that resulted in the Tar Heels holding a 13-6 record over the Red and White from over that same period. The reason for such uneven scheduling? "[I]t would be a disavantage to the Wolfpack financially to keep the game in Raleigh," Durham columnist Elton Casey explained; "it would lose money" [1]. They had to prioritize financial longevity over their on-field success.

Though a new stadium came in 1966, it had been a long time coming for Edwards, who arrived at the school in 1954. Speaking to the Wolfpack Club in 1962, Earle described his team as "football gypsies" before giving a lengthy discource about why NC State needed a new stadium: "A [new] stadium would open a whole new door for us. It would put us on a sound financial basis. It would be an aid in recruiting.... The earlier we ask for these things," he pleaded, "the better chance we have of getting them." At the meeting, he suggested committees be formed immediately to iron out details such as landscaping, parking, ticketing, and operations. The club was seeking to raise $60,000 (just over $600,000 in 2023) from private sources for the construction of such a stadium [2] [3].

Unfortunately, as Edwards alluded, these things take some planning. In the meantime, the university began to discover that the administrators were not the only people at NC State who wanted a new venue for their home games. During the 1963 homecoming game against Virginia Tech (a 13-7 win at which Riddick's old bleachers were burdened with a 20,500 person, over-capacity crowd), the student body made their opinions known: chants of "We want a new stadium" reverberated through the air during the game's final minutes [4].

To their credit, NC State was not wasting time -- yet. The state Consolidated University Board had only authorized the school to proceed on April 11th, 1963, with initial approval coming for a $2 million (just under $20 million in 2023) stadium with room for 40,000, a quarter of which was student seating. Much of the funding was expected to be raised via increased ticket sales and a $2 per person increase in students' annual athletic fees to $17 per year. Early plans called for the stadium to be built on 100 acres of college-owned property located "one half mile west of the State Fair grounds and south of the Camp Polk prison grounds," near the current location of the North Carolina Museum of Art, though by September it was announced that the likely location would be moving closer to the Fairgrounds than originally planned; this is where the stadium ultimately was located [5] [6]. (The land on which Carter-Finley now sits was originally slated for agricultural use as a research station and fish hatchery, while the original spot near Polk prison seems to have needed quite a lot of work; the preliminary study deemed the original grounds as "not an acceptable solution" for reason of cost by the Board of Trustees' Building Committee [7] [34]).

By September 1964, the dream became more of a reality when brothers Harry C. and Wilbert J. "Nick" Carter donated $200,000, in conjunction with a donation of $125,000 borrowed by the Wolfpack Club, of the $2 million goal. Brothers from Wallace, North Carolina, the Carters were NC State alumni with major roles running the Greensboro operations of the nationally-prominent J. P. Stevens' textile operations, where they opened one of the nation's first synthetic textile factories in 1937. Nick graduated from NC State in 1924, while Harry was a member of the class of 1932 who was conferred an honorary doctorate in 1943 for his work in supporting NC State's School of Textiles. Though still a long ways short of the school's goal, the brothers were recognized "because of their outstanding contributions... and the family to the textile industry of North Carolina and the nation over more than four decades," as Chancellor John Tyler Caldwell put it. Though it was ambitious, the school hoped that if such donations continued to roll in they would be ready to host games by 1965 rather than the original 1966 date [9] [10].

Construction work started about a month after the Carters' donation, with the drainage of a lake -- a aforementioned research hatchery -- near the center of the future stadium location coming as one of the first tasks at hand. Edwards, known for his laconic witticisms, lamented his team's recent two-game skid by referencing the lake's future drainage when he quipped "If we don't play any better, you'd better not drain it too soon because I'm going to jump in it" [11]. About two months later, on December 14th, the premises were honored with a groundbreaking ceremony [12].

Now is a good time to recall that NC State had pledged to open their new stadium in time for the 1965 season; given the way the construction timeframe had shaken out, this would have allowed just 9 months of construction time from the groundbreaking ceremony to the stadium's opening. Nonetheless, NC State athletics managers had already convinced the other three "Big Four" (aka Tobacco Road) teams to play their home games on NC State's campus for the first time since 1955. The incentive for NC State was probably to bolster 1965 season tickets under a program which gave ticket preference and priority to previous season ticket holders when picking seats at the new Carter Stadium in 1966 [13]. In other words, a short-term loss with the goal of long-term financial gain. The risk was doubled, however, because at that time it wasn't immediately clear if Carter Stadium would even be ready by 1966 [14].

On November 13th, 1965, NC State played its final football game in Riddick Stadium. A 3-0 win over Florida State for homecoming, most would find the moment bittersweet; David Grady, a halfback for NC State in 1907*, was not. "It's a damn good thing," Grady sneered when asked about Riddick's closure. "Should have happened thirty years ago. Was never big enough to start with." John W. Sexton, who saw time primarily as a substitute in 1908, was a bit more reflective, though still couched in his remarks, saying "I'm sorry in a way to see the stadium go. I hate to lose the name of Dr. Riddick" [16].

In a similar vein, NC State officials likely had bittersweet moments about their timeline for starting construction. Though they waited to begin until sufficient sums had been secured, rising costs of materials, in conjunction with higher bid prices than anticipated, resulted in the project being roughly $1 million over-budget in November 1965 [17]. However, in this case, the juice was worth the squeeze: NC State incurred so much debt in constructing Riddick Stadium that they were able to burn the last $3,000 worth of bonds at the under-construction Carter Stadium [18]. Around the same time, bids started for the construction of the A. E. Finley Fieldhouse, which was to be located at the non-grass berm end of the field [19].

By the time the complex was finished, the whole project cost $3.7 million (over $34.5 million in 2023) [21]. The double-deckered concrete stadium was complete with an enclosed press box, elevators, lighting, and paved parking options, among other options. Though still nearing completion into late September, the stadium managed to open with 41,000 seats in proper time on October 8th, 1966 [22]. Additionally, it was designed with the ability to expand to 70,000 if and when needed [25].

Edwards was enamored with the new venue. "I can't say enough about it," he raved. "How long we looked forward to this... What it will mean to our program -- recruiting, finances, making a new atmostphere. This gives us an athletic complex that ranks with anybody for miles around.... When I say we're glad to have it," he finished, "it's the understatement of the year -- and many more years" [26]. In the days leading up to the game, the Wolfpack rung in the new stadium quite literally: with a four day round-the-clock vigil, ringing the bell every 20 seconds. The so-called "Victory Bell" was a donation from the Southern Railroad when they discontinued steam rail service some time around 1954, and had been painted red in the school's colors. the 250-pound bell's toll could be heard almost a quarter mile away [27]. Sadly, the Victory Bell's tolling fell on deaf ears, as the Wolfpack lost the inaugural game 21-31.

When Carter Stadium officially opened on October 8th, 1966, it was the first time the Atlantic Coast Conference had ever seen a new football venue; the last time any member school had constructed on was Maryland's Byrd Stadium (now SECU Stadium) in 1950, three years prior to the creation of the conference. And it was not without its wrinkles: one of the most confusing aspects of the new stadium was that the upper and lower levels were numbered the same; though the tickets specified whether they were for the upper or lower level, it was only in one place, and if the ticket agent took the wrong stub of the ticket, it was impossible for the ticket holder to know which level they were meant to sit on. Though avoidable, such a gaffe is not surprising considering the old Riddick Stadium was just a single level. "We'll correct this next year," promised assistant athletic director Willis Casey, "by giving the upper level sections different numbers" [28].

The only thing missing on opening day -- besides, of course, a win over the Gamecocks -- was lighting for night games. Though originally included in stadium construction plans, the project's rising costs led the school to delay light installation until the following year. (Another cost-saving change was the decision to make only 10,000 of the seats the backed style rather than benches, rather than the entire venue) [7]. Construction on the lights began in March 1967, and NC State's field was once again night-time ready by mid-June 1967 [29] [31]. Notably, 1966 was the only season NC State has been unable to host a night football game since NC State organized the state's first such major intercollegiate football game back in 1930. That made NC State somewhat of an oddity in the state: though Wake Forest had permanent lighting at both Bowman Gray and Grove Stadium (built with lights in 1968), Wallace Wade lacked permanent lighting until 1984, and Kenan Memorial Stadium held out until 1988.

The first major renovation after the installation of permanent lighting was the expansion of the stadium, which was begun in 1974 and finished in the summer of 1975. Details on the work are scarce; it is not mentioned in any of the 3 major NC State athletics history books (The Wolfpack by Bill Beezley, Go Wolfpack! by Thad Mumau, or Touchdown Wolfpack by Douglas Herakovich), nor did I find any mention of it in the digitized pages of the Board of Trustees Meeting Buildings and Property Meeting Minutes. It appears as if the change was to replace all of the seating, some (or all) of which was wooden, with aluminum metal bleachers. This work increased the stadium's capacity to 44,000 [32]. It's not immediately clear to me how exactly the change in seating resulted in more seats, though it's possible some other work took place during the renovation that went unreported. Starting in 1978, the figure for a sellout started being reported as 45,600, but it's my impression that this merely includes standing room on the grass berm in the venue's total size.

Around that same time, the stadium was going through another major change, though this change was of a less material nature. Insteadl, the 13-year-old stadium was being renamed. Philanthropist A. E. Finley, for whom the stadium's original fieldhouse was named, had donated quite a large sum of money to the school over the years. Though he shunned public acclaim for his contributions, by 1979, it was conservatively estimated the former businessman had donated at least $1 million (just over $4 million in 2023) to the school. "I never broadcast what I do," Finley said. "If folks appreciate it, let them talk about it."

Finley's personal assistant, R. C. "Bobby" Brown, echoed Finley's unscrupulous generosity when he noted "I have no idea what [his donations] would add up to, and I'm sure he doesn't either." In fact, Finley had no idea the stadium was even being renamed in his honor. "They called me the day before [the dedication] and asked me to be sure to be at my seat a half hour before kickoff" [40]. The stadium was renamed Carter-Finley Stadium prior to the 9/8/1979 game against East Carolina; NC State went on to win 34-20.

Beyond the new name, initial upgrades to the facility were fairly minor for the same reason construction was delayed: they wanted to wait until they had paid off the debts on their 40-year bonds. This was accomplished in July 1978, nearly 26 years ahead of schedule. Among the prominent improvements planned for Carter-Finley upon the completion of their debts were the expansion of venue parking [33] [34]. Another scheme developed around this time was a proposal to convert Cater-Finley Stadium into an NFL-ready stadium by expanding of the stadium to its maximum capacity and adding a retractable roof; see a sketch of this below. The plans were nixed nearly immediately by NC State personnel due to the prohibitive costs -- it was described as "a terrible financial burden" [34] [35] [36].

Wikipedia and some period newspaper reports indicate a couple of capacity increases from in the 1980s and 1990s, claiming that the stadium's capacity expanded from 45,600 to 47,000 between 1983 and 1984 and to 51,500 from 1991 to 1992, however, no renovations actually ocurred. Though newspaper coverage did sometimes report increased capacities in those years, it was sporadic and inconsistent, and likely attempted to reflect overflow capacities the Wolfpack were allowing into sell-out games. A newspaper report in 1991 clarified the stadium's seated capacity was 47,000 plus another 6,000 seats on the grassy berm, which seems to align pretty closely with reports from the 1980s [38]. As Jimmy Bass, director of the Wolfpack Club from 2005-2005 put it, "Other than a few coats of paint, Carter-Finley has not gone through any renovations" [39].

One of the few actual changes to the stadium in this period was the erection of a new scoreboard before the 1985 season. The $500,000 (just under $1.5 million in 2023) system was computerized and fully-colored, comparable to a couple of modern scoreboards used by the NFL's Tampa Bay Buccaneers and Washington Redskins [42]. The improved board was financed entirely by sponsors such as Bobby Murray Chevrolet (now Capital Chevrolet), First Union National Bank, and Coca-Cola [43]. Joking on its cost and flashiness, Technician ran an article following the screen's debut from the perspective an imaginary, bewildered freshman: "You see, State's got this brand new scoreboard at their football stadium that is really out of this world," Joe College raves. "It's like a TV, I guess, because it shows these scenes on a screen when something good happens on the field... There's only one problem -- usually nothing's happening on the field, so they just show commercials instead (just like real TV!) [47].

After years of few modifications of subsequence, things quickly changed: in 2001, an ambitious multi-phase construction project was announced. First on the slate was the replacement of the grass berm at north end of the stadium with 5,864 permanent seats, including 2,030 chair back seats with cup holders, 3,710 bleacher seats, and 62 wheelchair locations with 62 companion seats. With the University eyeing a completion date of December 2002 or January 2003, they also expected to replace the 1985 scoreboard with a 160-foot-tall video board, as well as add three lighted practice fields on the campus complex. The final element of the initial phase of work was a 4-story, 100,000-square-foot football operations center which came to be known as the Wendell Murphy Football Center.
