9 In early football, after a team was forced to make a touchback, that team was required to kick the ball away in a play called a kickout from the yardage indicated above; this period is marked in tan. This remained unchanged until the 1909 season, when it was announced that teams would have the option perform a kickout or run a play from scrimmage; this section is marked in light red. The touchback as we know it today is uncolored.
10 Touchbacks remained at the 25-yard line unless the touchback was the result of a missed dropkick attempted on first down within the 25 yard line, in which case the ball was placed at the 10. This exception was removed before the 1906 season.
11 For the 1912-1913 football seasons, teams had the choice of performing a kickout from the 20 yard line or running a play from scrimmage from the 25. The kickout was eliminated before the 1914 football season.
12 25 yards for touchbacks on a kickoff or a free kick after a safety only.
13 Same as 2012 rule change except fair catches on kickoffs or on free kicks after a safety located between the receiving team's goal and 25-yard line are also counted for 25.
14 Formerly known as either a Try-For-Point or Try-At-Point; better known as "extra point" or "two-point conversion." Eliminated "punt-out" and "try-at-goal" techniques used from 1877-1921. When the runner crossed the goal line before 1922, they would mark with their foot the spot they broke the goal line. From there, the team had two choices to score: first, the team could perform a "try-at-goal," which allowed the ball to be brought out in a straight line from where the mark was to whatever spot the kicker preferred along that line. In doing this play, the ball would be held for a placekick, and the defense had to position themselves behind the goal line until the ball touched the ground. The second choice was the "punt-out" (sometimes called the kick out; not to be confused with the play used after a touchback by the same name), which was normally only used if the touchdown was uncomfortably close to the sideline. To do this, the runner who made the touchdown would drop back behind the goal line and punt the ball to a team mate who was waiting on the other side of the goal posts. That second team mate had to signal for a fair catch and mark the spot he caught the ball with his heel. By doing this, the team who scored the touchdown could improve their chances of scoring points after the touchdown. The risk of the punt-out was that if the fair catch failed, the team punting out forfeited their ability to earn more points on the play.
15 Before 1958, tries could only be worth 1 point; starting in 1958, teams had the chance to attempt a 2-point try in addition to the standard 1-point kick.
